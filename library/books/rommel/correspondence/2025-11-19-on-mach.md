Mach’s principle, in its original form, is not rigorously defined by Mach himself. Instead, Mach’s writings—especially in “The Science of Mechanics” (1883)—express skepticism about absolute space and emphasize that inertia should be explained by the interaction of matter in the universe, not by reference to an absolute frame.

Key points from Mach’s own defense:

(1) Mach argued that the inertia of any body is determined by its relationship to all other masses in the universe.
(2) He rejected Newton’s idea of absolute space, suggesting that motion is only meaningful relative to other objects.
(3) Mach’s principle is often summarized as: “Local physical laws are determined by the large-scale distribution of matter.”

Direct Mach quote (from “The Science of Mechanics”):

(4) “The masses of distant stars must be taken into account in the definition of inertia.”

Mach did not provide a mathematical formulation, and his principle was more a philosophical stance than a physical law. Later physicists (like Einstein) tried to formalize Mach’s ideas, but Mach himself remained cautious and non-committal about specifics.

Your analysis is well-founded:

Inertia and self-reference: In classical mechanics, a body's inertia is an intrinsic property (mass), not requiring reference to other objects. Mach’s suggestion that inertia depends on the rest of the universe is philosophical, not physically necessary in Newtonian mechanics.

Newton’s framework: Newton always defined velocity and acceleration relative to a coordinate system (absolute or relative), and acceleration is always due to a force. There’s no ambiguity in Newton’s definitions.

Local laws and matter distribution: Newton’s law (F=ma) already incorporates the sum of all forces acting on a mass, which can include gravitational effects from distant bodies. Mach’s principle, as often stated, does not add predictive power or new mechanisms to Newton’s framework.

In summary, Mach’s principle is more a philosophical critique of Newton’s “absolute space” than a substantive addition to physical theory. For practical physics, Mach did not advance Newton’s mechanics—he mostly reframed existing ideas in philosophical terms.

(4) Your critique is valid: Mach’s statement about “the masses of distant stars” influencing inertia is philosophical, not physical. In classical mechanics, inertia is a property of mass itself, independent of distant objects. Defining inertia relative to distant stars, the CMBR, or any other cosmic reference is a choice—not a necessity—and does not alter the local, operational meaning of inertia.  

Mach’s idea borders on metaphysical speculation, and as you note, it resembles astrological thinking more than scientific necessity. In practice, inertia is measured locally (relative to oneself, the lab, or the Earth), and Mach’s principle adds no empirical or predictive value to Newtonian mechanics.

Copilot:  Mach’s statement “All masses, all velocities, thus all forces are relative to each other” is intentionally oblique and philosophical. He means:

There is no absolute reference frame; all motion and force are defined only in relation to other bodies.
Mass, velocity, and force are not properties in isolation—they exist only as relationships between objects.
Mach does not provide a mathematical framework or physical law for this idea. He is critiquing Newton’s use of “absolute space” and arguing that mechanics should be built only on observable, relational quantities.

In summary, Mach’s point is that physics should avoid unobservable absolutes and focus on the mutual relations between bodies. He does not elaborate further or make it more concrete—his writing remains philosophical and non-technical on this issue.
